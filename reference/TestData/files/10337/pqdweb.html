The Song of Hannah: Text and contexts
by Cook, Joan Elizabeth, Ph.D., Vanderbilt University, 1989, 228 pages; AAT 8919684
Abstract (Summary)

The Song of Hannah (1 Sam 2:1-10) contains several key features that invite study from various perspectives. The project addresses these: the issue of the best available text, its dates of composition and of insertion into the book of Samuel, its genre and poetic features; and on a larger scale the Song's interaction with its narrative context and the appearance of its archetypal "barren mother" imagery in Second Isaiah. Methodologically, the study applies text-critical principles to the various versions of the Song, and compares its linguistic and thematic features with those of archaic poems and Deuteronomistic passages in Joshua-2 Kings. It then analyzes form-critical aspects of the Song and examines its poetic features. Then the project applies typic and thematic approaches to the Song's immediate and larger contexts: to the birth-of-Samuel account in 1 Sam 1:1-2:21, to 1 and 2 Samuel, and to Isaiah 40-55. The study concludes that the Song, a general hymn of praise, may well have been composed as early as the tenth century B.C.E., and was inserted into the narrative toward the end of the monarchy by editors with priestly interests. The Song's position early in the books of Samuel, and its theme of divine protection of the needy during times of transition, using "barren mother" imagery, celebrates the ultimate rule of the Deity in the lives of individuals and in affairs of state, and sets the theocratic tone for the incipient monarchy. In a later transitional time, Second Isaiah's archetypal "barren mother" imagery celebrates the divine protection of Jerusalem on the verge of return to the Land at the end of the exile. These conclusions suggest that further study of later versions and appropriations of the Song, such as in Targum Jonathan, Pseudo-Philo and the Magnificat (Luke 1:46-55), would demonstrate the continuing significance of both theme and imagery.
